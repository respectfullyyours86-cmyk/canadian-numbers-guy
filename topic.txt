Podcast Script: The May CPI "Headfake"
(Music: Quick, high-energy transition into your voice)
The May CPI dropped this morning: 3.2% year-over-year.
The street called 3.0%. We came in 20 basis points hotter. And right on cue, the 'Inflation is back' takes are already writing themselves.
Here's the reality: they're looking at the noise. I'm looking at the signal.
That headline number is being pulled by one thing — gasoline, up 33.2% year-over-year in May. Third consecutive month of energy-driven acceleration. The reason is specific: the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint that handles roughly a fifth of global oil supply, shut down by the ongoing Middle East conflict. This is not a domestic spending problem. This is not Canadian consumers going reckless. This is a geography problem on the other side of the planet.
Now here's where the media gets it wrong. Strip out gasoline, and CPI rises 2.2% — up modestly from 2.0% in April, which is worth noting. But the number that actually guides Bank of Canada decisions? CPI-trim sits at 2.0%. CPI-median at 2.1%. Those are the BoC's two preferred core measures. Both unchanged from April. Both right on target.
And here's the piece almost nobody is going to mention today: Canada is in a technical recession. Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP. Q4 2025 contracted at an annualized 1.0%. Q1 2026 contracted again. Unemployment is sitting between 6.5 and 7 percent. The largest component of the CPI basket — shelter, at roughly 30% of the index — has been decelerating for over a year. Mortgage costs have fallen year-over-year for thirty-three straight months.
Now, I'll be precise: not everything outside energy is clean. Grocery prices are running hot. Fresh vegetables up 9.0%. Tomatoes up 45.2% year-over-year — because of supply contractions in Mexico tied to US tariff policy and bad weather. But the Bank of Canada is not going to hike rates to bring down tomato prices caused by Mexican farm policy. That's not how monetary policy works. That's the noise.
The takeaway for your portfolio and your wallet: the 'hike-now' narrative has no foundation in the numbers the BoC actually uses. CPI-trim says 2.0%. The economy is in contraction. The energy spike is geopolitical and three months running. Bond markets agree — they're pricing a 7% probability of a July hike. Seven percent.
I'm watching July 15th. I'm tracking the core. And I'm ignoring the gas pump.
Stay analytical. See you next time."
